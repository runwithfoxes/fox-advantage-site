# Chapter 64b: The robot stand-up

_Part 4_


My team meeting ran for three and a half minutes. It happened while I was making tea. There was nobody in the room. I wasn't in the room either, because there was no room, and I wasn't invited in any case. But it did happen - Klara, Jo and Nat had a good ole debate, "disagreed and committed" like all good teams should.

Sounds odd but it is the result of something I've been tinkering away at for a bit. I wanted to see if my AI team could do what good teams do - work, debate and ideally agree on priorities, without me there to drive the discussion.

I've changed client details, but this is what minutes from their meetings look like when I go check:




To backtrack a bit - Klara, Jo and Nat are agents. My agents. A project manager, a growth lead, and a finance one.

They each run on their own role file with their own brief and their own stubbornness, and every morning after the briefing pipeline finishes, they sit down with each other and argue about what the top seven on my briefing page should be. I insisted I couldn't handle more than seven.

So when I come back to the kitchen, the list is there. A JSON file with a trade history attached.

They each share priorities from the things they are tasked to do. For example, Klara wanted a whole bunch of items to put in front of me, all tasks for a big outbound client launch thirty days away. It wanted me to review, get my act together and sort out the email domains, go over a walk-through plan for the messages. It also had written feedback it believed I owe a client, which it spotted in a meeting transcript from a couple days ago.




Jo pushed back. It had two replies sitting in my gmail inbox from warm contacts, both landed on Thursday, both now at day three of the seventy-two hour window it treats as sacred. Growth execs take these windows very seriously, even the robot ones.

Apparently my average time to reply is about four days, thus the perceived urgency. And it wanted Klara's feedback item off the list, because I talk to that client nearly every day, so it's not a thing to worry about.

Nat doesn't care about replies, ever. Nat cares about invoices. It puts the biggest unpaid line in the book on the (virtual) table and reminds everyone that Paul is shocking at getting invoices out. On average I take about twenty days to get around to sending one, and Nat has the data to prove it. That is fair. I've never been a big fan of admin tasks - thus perhaps my enthusiasm for building these systems.




Klara is the decision-maker within this mighty AI team. I generally don't play favourites among the robots but somebody needs to do it, and in fairness, Klara is a project manager. Says it in its role description. So it decided what gets paused and what gets into my top 7. Nat's two unpaid invoices get pushed to tomorrow, because nobody knows how old they are. Including me I suspect.

The final list goes to my /today briefing page with the trade log attached.

It all sounds like fiction, and I'm not going to claim three agents arguing produces more accurate answers than one well-prompted call. But I get my list of 7 things to focus on today. That said, writing this wasn't on it.
