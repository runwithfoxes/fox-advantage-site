# The gift in the card

_Part 4_

The National Lottery guaranteed someone a million euros every New Year's Day. The ads were always warm, funny, well made. They told people to imagine winning. And for years, the tickets never sold out. Not because people didn't want to dream. They needed a reason to walk up to the counter in Spar during the second week of December and spend twenty quid on something to put inside a Christmas card for their brother.

The previous ads had positioned the Millionaire Raffle as a thing you might win. They never positioned it as a gift.

Now, we'd been doing work on mental availability. If you've read Jenni Romaniuk or spent any time with the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute's research, you'll know the concept. Category Entry Points, the specific situations where someone thinks about your category and, hopefully, your brand. Not "who is the target audience." More useful than that. When do they buy. Why. Where are they. What are they doing at the time.

We worked with RedC to map ours. Here's what made it interesting. The National Lottery is not the obvious gifting brand. But it's the perfect gift that feels thoughtful for your brother, cousin, your gran. The insight is we were not just about gifting, but a gift to go inside a card. That was the CEP doubled down on. "Gift to put in a card" was specific enough to brief against, and broad enough to generate real sales.

So in 2020 we made a new Millionaire Raffle campaign. Full credit to Damien O'Donnell, the most talented film director I know. Every frame links the brand to the gifting occasion. Damien even managed to get "tickets are limited" into the script without it feeling forced, which is a minor miracle if you've ever tried to get a compliance line into a piece of film.

The results were immediate. For the first year in many, we sold out all the tickets. We had to pull the advertising. Not "we optimised the media plan." We pulled it. Because we ran out of things to sell. The ROI was 27:1.

It sold out the following year too. And the year after that. When I left, my only parting advice was not to change this campaign until there's evidence it is no longer working. 

The product didn't change. The media budget didn't double. The audience was the same people, in the same shops, walking past the same counter. What changed was the connection between the brand and a specific buying situation. We stopped reminding people the raffle existed and started showing up in the moment they were already reaching for their wallet.

It wasn't a fluke. It was proper marketing work. The fundamentals. The CEP mapping, the research, the interviews with real customers. It took months. But it led us to the perhaps blindingly obvious insight. That in the second week of December, people all over the country are standing in Spar looking at a card rack, wondering if a twenty euro note tucked inside a Christmas card exposes their lack of thoughtfulness this year. It does. That's a CEP. 

The conversation about AI in marketing has mostly been about what the tools can do. The more interesting question is what you're feeding them. If you haven't mapped the entry points, the machine just generates more stuff. AI doesn't fix weak marketing. It scales it. I love the potential of AI, but you've got to do the reps and learn the fundamentals.
