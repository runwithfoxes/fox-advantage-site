Research
AI + human
AWhere do you show up in AI search?
When someone asks ChatGPT or Claude or Perplexity to recommend a company like yours, do you show up? Most companies have no idea. They're optimising for Google but the buying behaviour is shifting to AI search and they're invisible there.

We built a GEO audit that tests this. Give us your brand and URL, we generate 25 real buyer-intent prompts across five patterns, the kinds of questions your prospects are actually asking. Then we check which brands get cited, which get recommended, and which don't appear at all. You get a scored report showing your citation rate, who's winning the prompts you're losing, and a prioritised fix list to close the gaps.

BA research agent that learns what you care about
We built an agent that goes out each week on its own and finds material across YouTube, Substack and other sources on the topics that matter to you. It identifies the key ideas, writes them up, and puts them into a folder for you to review.

The ones you like, it learns from. The ones you skip, it learns from too. Over time it gets better at knowing what's signal and what's noise for your specific interests. It builds and learns on its own. You just review what it finds.

This saves hours of reading time every week, and the quality improves the longer you use it.
Research agent output visual goes here
CFind the conversations that matter on Reddit
Reddit is where people ask real questions about real problems. We built a tool that monitors the subreddits that matter to your business, finds the posts where someone is asking about exactly the kind of thing you solve, and drafts a helpful response.

It doesn't post anything. It queues the responses for a human to review, edit if needed, and post themselves. The human is in the process where it matters, the tone, the timing, the judgment call on whether this is the right conversation to join.

We used this for a client selling showers. The tool watched five subreddits, found posts asking about water pressure, electric showers, replacements. It drafted technically accurate responses in the right voice. The team reviewed and posted the ones that fit. Genuine help, not spam.

It's a starting point. Once you can see the shape of what you're measuring, you can start managing the brand properly.

Marketing ops
AI + human
AA project manager that actually runs your morning
We built a project manager agent that gets you from "just opened the laptop" to "I know what to do today" in under two minutes.

It runs in two passes. First, the scan. It reads your ranked priorities, email index, active client status, tasks, calendar, and any new meeting transcripts. It shows you your top priorities for the day with a reason for each, emails waiting from people you actually know, what got built or left unfinished yesterday, and new transcripts.

Then it asks which ones you want it to read properly.

Second pass is the deep read. If you pick items, it reads them in full, every word, not a preview, and comes back with concrete proposals. Move a priority, add a task, park something, or no change. It never silently rearranges your day. It surfaces the evidence and lets you decide.

Without this, your morning is twenty minutes of scrolling through email, calendar, tasks, session logs, trying to remember what you committed to and what's overdue. By the time you've done all that, your focus is shot and you've already reacted to three things you shouldn't have prioritised. This compresses that into a ranked list with reasons, and the day starts decided, not reactive.

And because it talks to the other agents through a shared briefing board, they don't cross wires. The growth agent won't cold-message a client who's mid-delivery. The finance agent will know when a milestone hit so it can prepare an invoice. You get a team that actually coordinates, not three assistants reading the same inbox.

BA commercial director that keeps the money visible
Marketing spends. Without a commercial counterweight, it spends on whichever activity feels busy. We built a commercial director agent that owns the money side of the operation.

It tracks revenue targets, pricing, capacity, invoicing, tax deadlines, and cash. It turns pipeline into expected revenue at probability, so campaign choices connect to money, not vanity. It tracks cost per reply, cost per accepted connection, and cost per won deal, so campaign spend gets judged on commercial output, not impressions.

It doesn't send invoices or file returns. It drafts invoices when a delivery milestone is hit, chases overdue payments with a draft email for you to send, warns when you're overcommitted on capacity or behind on target, and flags tax deadlines before they become emergencies.

And because it reads the same briefing board as the project manager and the growth agent, it stays coordinated. When the project manager logs a milestone, the commercial director queues the invoice. When the growth agent logs a campaign cost, the commercial director sees it against cash. When cash is tight, the growth agent knows to hold off on paid tools.

A marketing function without a commercial director drifts toward activity. With one, every campaign has to answer a money question eventually.
