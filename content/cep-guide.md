# How to run a Category Entry Points survey

## A practical guide to measuring mental availability

---

## 1. What we're actually measuring (and why it matters)

Someone in your category is going to buy something this week. The question is whether your brand shows up in their head when they reach for their wallet.

That's mental availability. Not whether people like you. Not whether they've heard of you. Whether they think of you, unprompted, in the moment that matters.

Jenni Romaniuk at the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute put it simply: the most important search engine is still the one in your mind. Purchases start with memory, not Google. We reach into our heads first, and we only reach for the phone when our heads come up empty.

Category Entry Points are the building blocks. They're the specific situations, needs, emotions, and triggers that lead someone to think about your category. Not your brand. The category. "I need to get back in shape." "We need a new supplier before the contract expires." "I want something to put inside this birthday card." Those are CEPs.

Your brand either shows up in those moments or it doesn't. And you can measure exactly how often it does, across how many of those moments, and whether you're gaining or losing ground to competitors.

This guide walks through the whole process. How to find your CEPs, how to decide which ones matter, how to design and run the survey, how to calculate the three metrics that tell you where you stand, and what to do with what you find.

It's not theory. We've done this work with clients, and the methodology comes from Romaniuk's research at the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute. If you want the academic depth, her book *Better Brand Health* is where to go. What follows is the practical version. The stuff you need to brief a research agency and make sense of what comes back.

---

## 2. Find your Category Entry Points

Before you can measure anything, you need a list of the buying situations that matter in your category. This is the bit where teams often go wrong, because they start with their brand positioning and work backwards. That's the wrong direction. CEPs exist whether your brand does or not.

### The W's framework

Romaniuk developed a framework of seven W's to help structure the brainstorm. Think of them as lenses, not boxes. Each one surfaces a different kind of buying trigger.

**Why** is about motives. Why does someone enter the category in the first place? In B2C, it might be "I want to treat myself" or "I need a quick pick-me-up." In B2B, it might be "to increase productivity" or "to help demonstrate the company is a good corporate citizen."

**When** is timing. The weekend, after payday, end of financial year, during a digital transformation, when a new CMO arrives and needs quick wins. Timing triggers are often overlooked because they feel obvious, but they're powerful precisely because they're tied to real calendar moments you can plan around.

**Where** is location. At a supermarket checkout, at home, online, working from home versus the office, international versus local. In a B2B context, this includes whether the buyer's team is co-located or distributed, and whether the decision is being made at head office or a regional branch.

**While** refers to what's happening around the purchase. Before, during, or after. Launching a new product, dealing with a seasonal demand spike, responding to a competitor move. These co-activities shape which brands feel safe and which feel risky.

**With what** is about adjacent categories. What else are they buying or using alongside yours? In B2B, this is particularly important because products need to integrate with existing systems. "Works with our accounting software" is a CEP.

**With/for whom** is about the other people involved. In B2B, this is where buying committees live. Will the board approve? Will junior staff find it easy to use? Will it make someone look good to their boss? In B2C, it's who the purchase is for: a gift for your brother, a treat shared with friends, something the kids will eat.

**How feeling** covers the emotional context. Stressed, bored, excited, anxious about making the wrong choice, wanting to feel like they're excelling at their job. Emotions before buying, during buying, and the desired emotional outcome after.

### How to run the brainstorm

Get the right people in the room. That means marketers, salespeople, customer service staff, and ideally a few actual customers. Salespeople are gold for this because they hear the buying triggers daily, in the customer's own words.

Work through each W systematically. Use them as prompts, not templates. For every W, ask: what are the situations where someone thinks about our category? Write everything down. Don't filter yet.

In B2B, consider each W from two angles. The business perspective (objectives, characteristics, challenges) and the professional perspective (the decision-maker as a person with their own career ambitions, fears, and politics). "A company that the board would respect" is a different CEP from "makes a tedious task easier to do," and both are valid.

You're aiming for somewhere between 15 and 30 candidate CEPs before filtering. If you have fewer than 10, you haven't pushed hard enough. If you have more than 40, you're probably getting too granular.

### The language rule

This matters enough to get its own heading. CEPs must be written in customer language, not marketing language.

"I want to get back in shape" is a CEP.
"Seeking fitness transformation solutions" is not. It's a marketing department talking to itself.

"I need to lose weight for my wedding" is a CEP.
"Event-based weight management goals" is what happens when someone rewrites a real insight until all the life has been squeezed out.

"I want to work out at home" is a CEP.
"Convenient home-based fitness solutions" is a press release.

If you can't imagine a real person saying it to a friend, rewrite it until you can.

## 3. Decide which CEPs matter

You'll have a long list. You can't invest in all of them. The next step is filtering down to a shortlist of five to eight CEPs that represent real opportunities for your brand. Romaniuk calls this the "long-short list."

### The three C's

Three forces determine whether a CEP is worth investing in.

**Credibility** asks: can your brand credibly deliver on this CEP? This has two parts. External credibility is what customers already believe about you. If buyers don't currently associate you with "enterprise-grade security," that's a gap you'd need to close before messaging it. Internal credibility is what your product or service actually does. If there's no way your offering can fulfil the CEP, take it off the list.

The way to test external credibility is through the survey itself, which we'll get to. You present buyers with a CEP and a list of brands, and they tell you which brands they associate with each situation. If your brand scores significantly higher than expected, you have a "mental advantage" on that CEP. If it scores significantly lower, you have a "mental disadvantage." The threshold is roughly plus or minus five percentage points from the expected score.

**Competitiveness** asks: how crowded is this mental space? If six competitors already have a mental advantage on a CEP, it's going to be expensive and slow to make headway. CEPs where few or no brands have established a mental advantage are easier to own.

This isn't a reason to avoid strong CEPs entirely. If the CEP is commercially important and you already have some presence, you might need to defend it. But for growth, look for the less contested spaces.

**Commonality** asks: how often does this buying situation actually happen, and how valuable is it when it does? In B2C, frequency is often the best proxy for value. A CEP that triggers weekly has more commercial potential than one that happens twice a year. In B2B, where deal sizes vary enormously, you need to weight frequency by typical deal value. Two CEPs might both trigger for 20% of buyers, but if one leads to contracts worth ten times the other, the commercial value is very different.

### The elimination process

Start with all your CEPs. Remove the ones that lack credibility, both external and internal. Remove the ones where competition is fierce and you have no existing foothold. From what's left, rank by commonality and commercial value.

The output is five to eight CEPs. These become your messaging priorities, the situations you want your brand to be linked to in buyer memory. Not all at once. You'll build links to different CEPs over time, one execution at a time, gradually widening your brand's memory network.

### Scoring it

A simple matrix works. For each CEP, score commonality, competitiveness, and credibility on a scale of 1 to 5. Total the scores. This doesn't replace judgement, but it forces the conversation and makes the trade-offs visible.

| CEP | Commonality | Competitiveness | Credibility | Total |
|-----|-------------|-----------------|-------------|-------|
| "When teams lose track of customer conversations" | 5 | 5 | 5 | 15 |
| "When customer issues require multiple teams to solve" | 5 | 4 | 5 | 14 |
| "When customer messages pile up faster than you can respond" | 5 | 5 | 4 | 14 |
| "When growth makes your current process harder to manage" | 4 | 4 | 4 | 12 |

That's a real example from a B2B software company's CEP prioritisation. The highest-scoring CEPs are the ones where the buying situation is common, competition hasn't fully claimed the space, and the brand can credibly deliver.

## 4. Design the survey

This is the section you came for. The survey is how you measure which brands buyers associate with which buying situations, and from that, you calculate your mental availability.

### Survey structure

A full CEP tracking survey has several components. Not all are essential for a first-time study, but here's the complete architecture.

**1. Classification and screening**

Standard demographics plus a screener to ensure you're surveying category buyers, not the general population. You want people who actually buy from your category, or who have bought recently. If you're selling enterprise software, you need decision-makers, not the general public.

Screen out people who work in market research, journalism, or PR, as they'll skew your results.

**2. Brand reaction (optional but useful)**

Before you get into CEPs, show respondents a list of brands and ask them to indicate, quickly and instinctively, which emotion they associate with each. This is a fast-response exercise, not a considered judgement. It gives you a baseline emotional reading that's independent of the CEP data.

**3. Spontaneous CEP association (this is the core)**

This is the question that does the heavy lifting. You present a buying situation and ask, open-ended, which brands come to mind.

The format: "When you [specific CEP situation], which brands come to mind?"

For example:
- "When you want to get back in shape, which fitness brands come to mind?"
- "When your team loses track of customer conversations, which software platforms come to mind?"
- "When you're looking for a gift to put inside a birthday card, which brands come to mind?"

Respondents type in whatever brands they think of. No prompting, no list. This measures true top-of-mind retrieval.

Each respondent sees roughly 10 CEPs, randomly selected and rotated from your full list. This keeps the survey manageable and gives you coverage across all your CEPs without exhausting anyone.

**4. Prompted CEP association**

Same situations again, but now with a list of brands to choose from. "Which of these brands do you associate with this situation?" Respondents can select as many or as few as they want, or none.

The gap between spontaneous and prompted tells you something important. High prompted but low spontaneous means people know you can do it, but you're not top of mind when the moment arrives. That's a media and messaging problem, not a product problem.

**5. Product-level association (if you have a portfolio)**

If your brand has multiple products or services, repeat the exercise at product level. "Which of these [brand] products would you consider for this situation?" This tells you whether buyers are linking the right products to the right moments.

**6. Last occasion (behavioural anchor)**

"The last time you [CEP situation], which brand did you actually buy?" This grounds the data in real behaviour, not just stated associations. The gap between "brands that come to mind" and "brands actually bought" is where physical availability (distribution, pricing, availability) shows up.

**7. Brand image attributes**

A standard set of image statements. "Which of these brands would you describe as trustworthy / innovative / good value / a brand for people like me?" This is supplementary to the CEP data but useful for understanding why certain brands win on certain CEPs.

**8. Usage frequency and recency**

How often they buy from the category, how recently they bought. This lets you segment your CEP data by heavy versus light buyers, which matters because light buyers are harder to reach and more important for growth.

**9. Attitudinal statements**

A set of agree/disagree statements about category behaviour and attitudes. "I tend to be loyal to brands I like." "Special offers make no difference to how often I buy." These are context, not core.

### Key design decisions

**How many CEPs to test.** Your full list might be 20 or 30, but each respondent sees only about 10, randomly selected. This keeps the survey under 15 minutes and gives you enough data across all CEPs. If you're running a smaller study, you might need to trim the list to your top 12-15 CEPs and show all of them.

**CEP wording.** Frame each CEP with an appropriate intro based on its W-type:
- Why: "When you want to..."
- Feeling: "When you are feeling..."
- When: "When it's..." or "When..."
- Where: "When you are at..." or "When you are..."

Keep the language natural. Remember: customer language, not marketing language.

**Brand list.** Include your brand and five to eight key competitors. Randomise the order. In the prompted sections, always include a "none of these" option.

**Sample size.** For a robust study: 500 to 1,000 category buyers. For a first-time study with a smaller budget: 300 can work, but you'll have less confidence at the individual CEP level. The bigger your competitor set, the larger the sample you need.

**Who to survey.** This is where teams regularly go wrong. You need category buyers, not your customers. If you only survey people who already buy from you, you'll massively overstate your mental availability. Use a research panel that recruits from the general population and screen for category usage.

**Survey length.** Aim for 10 to 15 minutes maximum. Longer surveys produce worse data. Respondent fatigue is real, and it shows up as random clicking and straight-lining.

### Common mistakes

Romaniuk has flagged several patterns that undermine the work:

**Rebadging old measures.** Some teams take their existing brand tracking questions and rename them "mental availability." That's not what this is. CEP-based measurement is structurally different from traditional awareness or consideration tracking.

**Using marketing language in the CEPs.** We said it before. It's the single easiest way to get bad data. If the respondent has to decode your jargon, you're measuring their comprehension, not their memory.

**Surveying your own customers.** Your customer database will tell you that you have great mental availability. Of course it will. They already buy from you. You need non-customers in the sample, ideally making up the majority.

**Testing too few CEPs.** If you only test three or four CEPs, you're not measuring mental availability. You're measuring awareness of a handful of associations. The whole point is to understand the breadth of your memory network.

**Not randomising.** Order effects are real. The first CEP and the first brand in a list get an unfair advantage. Randomise both.

## 5. Calculate the three metrics

Once the fieldwork is back, you calculate three numbers. Together, they tell you exactly where your brand stands in category buyer memory.

### Mental Penetration (MPen)

**What it measures:** The percentage of category buyers who link your brand to at least one CEP. It's the most basic measure: what proportion of the market has any memory connection to your brand at all?

**Formula:** (Number of unique people who linked your brand to any CEP / Total respondents) x 100

**Worked example:** 1,000 respondents surveyed. 380 unique people mentioned FitTracker for at least one CEP. Someone who mentioned FitTracker for three different CEPs still counts as one person.

FitTracker MPen = (380 / 1,000) x 100 = **38%**

For context, MyFitnessPal scored 62% and Peloton scored 48% in the same study. FitTracker has the lowest mental penetration of the three. Fewer people think of it at all.

**What it tells you:** Higher MPen means more people can retrieve your brand from memory when a buying situation arises. If your MPen is low, the immediate priority is reach: getting in front of more category buyers.

### Network Size (NS)

**What it measures:** Among people who do think of you, how many different CEPs do they link you to? This is the width of your memory network.

**Formula:** Total CEP links for your brand / Number of people who linked your brand to any CEP

**Worked example:** FitTracker received 1,900 total mentions across all CEPs. 380 people recalled FitTracker for at least one CEP.

FitTracker NS = 1,900 / 380 = **5.0 CEPs per person**

MyFitnessPal scored 4.66 and Peloton scored 3.92. FitTracker has the highest network size, meaning the people who do think of it, think of it across more buying situations.

**What it tells you:** A high NS means your existing associations are broad. But if MPen is low and NS is high, you have a small group of people who know you well, not a large group who know you at all. That's a common pattern for specialist or premium brands.

### Mental Market Share (MMS)

**What it measures:** Your brand's share of all the CEP-brand links in the entire category. This is the metric that correlates with actual market share over time.

**Formula:** (Your brand's total CEP links across all respondents / Total CEP links for all brands) x 100

**Worked example:** FitTracker had 1,900 total links. All brands combined had 8,580 total links.

FitTracker MMS = (1,900 / 8,580) x 100 = **22.1%**

MyFitnessPal scored 33.7% and Peloton scored 21.9%.

**What it tells you:** MMS is a leading indicator. Brands that build mental market share today tend to see actual market share follow 6 to 18 months later. If your MMS is higher than your sales market share, your brand building is working and sales should follow (assuming physical availability is decent). If MMS is lower than sales share, you're living off distribution and promotion, and that's vulnerable.

### Benchmarks

Across FMCG categories, the typical leader scores:
- MPen: around 81% (highest observed: 92%)
- NS: around 7.0 (highest: 10.2)
- MMS: around 14% (highest: 21%)

These are useful reference points, but your category will have its own dynamics. The more brands in the category, the lower the expected MMS for any single player.

## 6. Turn what you find into decisions

The data is only useful if it changes what you do. Here's how.

### Individual CEP analysis

Beyond the three summary metrics, look at your brand's performance on each individual CEP. This is where the strategic decisions live.

For each CEP, calculate what percentage of respondents linked your brand to it. Then compare with competitors.

**Defend** the CEPs where you're strongest. If 45% of respondents link you to "I need motivation to exercise" and no competitor is above 30%, that's a CEP worth protecting. Keep investing. Keep showing up in those moments.

**Attack** the CEPs where a competitor leads but the gap is closeable. If Peloton leads "I want to work out at home" at 62% and you're at 35%, that might be worth contesting, especially if the home fitness market is growing.

**Consider** the CEPs where a competitor has an overwhelming advantage and your credibility is low. Investing here might not be the best use of budget.

### Creative briefs

CEPs should change how you brief creative work. Instead of "build awareness" or "communicate our brand purpose," the brief becomes: build a mental link between our brand and this specific buying situation.

One CEP per execution. That's a discipline. Each ad, each piece of content, each campaign should connect your brand to one CEP clearly. Co-present the brand and the situation on the same page, screen, or audio. Romaniuk calls this co-presentation. It's how memory links get built.

Over time, across multiple executions, you build links to different CEPs. This is how brands widen their memory network. Not by cramming five messages into one ad, but by running a portfolio of executions, each focused on a different buying moment.

### Media planning

Map your priority CEPs to real-world moments and channels. If one of your CEPs is "when it's the end of financial year," your media weight should increase in March-April (or whenever financial year ends for your buyers). If it's "when I'm commuting," audio and mobile are more relevant than print.

### Campaign evaluation

Before running a campaign, identify which CEP it targets. After, measure whether brand-CEP linkage on that specific CEP moved. This is a more useful measure of campaign effectiveness than general awareness, because it tells you whether the campaign built the specific memory you intended.

### Portfolio gaps

Sometimes the CEP data reveals a situation where buyers have a clear need but your brand can't credibly deliver. That's a product gap, not a messaging gap. CEP research doesn't just inform communications. It can inform product development and service design.

## 7. Track it over time

Mental availability isn't a one-off measurement. It's a tracking metric.

### How often to measure

Run the survey every 6 to 12 months for most brands. Quarterly if you have the budget and you're making significant changes to your marketing activity. The Ehrenberg-Bass data shows that mental availability shifts are gradual, so monthly measurement is usually overkill unless you're in a fast-moving category.

### What to track

The three metrics over time: MPen, NS, and MMS. Plot them as time series. Compare with actual sales data. In our experience, MMS is the best leading indicator of future market share.

Also track individual CEP performance wave over wave. Are the CEPs you're investing in moving? Are competitors making gains on CEPs you thought you owned?

### Pre/post campaign measurement

If you're running a major campaign targeting a specific CEP, measure before and after. The question isn't "did awareness go up?" The question is: "did more buyers link our brand to this specific buying situation?"

### Memory decay

Brand-CEP links decay over time. Romaniuk's research shows that if you stop investing, competitors will gradually erode your position. Building mental availability is not a one-and-done project. It requires sustained investment, in the same way that physical availability requires ongoing distribution work.

The brands that grow are the ones that build wider, fresher memory networks, consistently, over time. More people thinking of them, across more buying situations, more recently.
